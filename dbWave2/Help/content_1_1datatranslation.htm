Analog-to-digital conversion

In order to acquire data, you need a data acquisition card that converts electrical signals into numbers.

dbWave is interfaced to Data Translation data acquisition cards through a generic driver from Data Translation called DTOpenLayer.

The driver is downloadable from DT website at http://www.datatranslation.com/cd/omni/

Basically, the driver is generic and provides the same program interface to a program, which means that you can use dbWave with a whole line of data acquisition cards. In our laboratory, we currently use USB cards called multifunction cards of the DT9800 family.

When you acquire data, you need to pay attention to 3 parameters:

  • How fast you sample your data. This is called the sampling rate. Ideally, the sampling rate should be at least 2 x as fast as the highest frequency of your signal (theorem of Shannon). Usually, we sample the signal at 10 kHz. If you don't sample fast enough, you may see phantom frequencies in your signal (due to undersampling). This also mean that even if you are not interested in very high frequencies, if your source signal contains high frequencies, you need to filter them using an analog filter before sampling them with your A/D card. In practice, we filter the incoming data with a 3 kHz filter.
  • How sensitive your data scale is. This is related to the fact that the electrical signal is converted into a number which usually is an integer. This means that if you data acquisition card has a 12 bits resolution, data are encoded between 0 and 4095. If you are using a 16 bits card, data are encoded between 0 and 65535. Since extracellular recorded spikes in insects are typically in the range of 100 µV to 10 mV, we have to amplify the signal before making the conversion, and store along the factor of conversion to be able to convert these numbers back to a a voltage amplitude.
  • How many channels you want to monitor. DT9800 cards typically monitor up to 16 channels which are sampled either sequentially or simultaneously. As the sampling frequency is usually given by a clock located on the A/D card, this may reduce the sampling per channel. Typically, we record 1 to 3 channels simultaneously so that it is not a problem for our application. As the data are stored sequentially, generally,  the data files contain data which are interleaved: t1: chan1, chan2, chan3, ...; t2: chan1, chan2, chan3, ..; etc.
